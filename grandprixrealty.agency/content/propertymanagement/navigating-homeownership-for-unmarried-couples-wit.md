  <h2>Choosing the Right Ownership Structure</h2>
  <p>Before you set your hearts on that urban loft or futuristic family home, you’ll need to hammer out how the property will legally belong to you both. Ownership structure is kind of like the foundation code of a building—it determines how your names will be linked to the property and what happens in the storm of unexpected life events, like breakups or the passing of a partner. For unmarried couples, the two main flavours for property ownership are <strong>joint tenancy</strong> and <strong>tenancy in common</strong>.</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Joint Tenancy:</strong> This option is designed for couples who see themselves as equal halves of a whole. Both of you will own the property equally, with a cool feature known as the <em>right of survivorship</em>. If one of you shuffles off this mortal coil, the ownership automatically transfers to the surviving partner. A joint tenancy requires you both to pony up equal shares of the property’s purchase at the same time, but it’s worth the security if you both value having equivalent stakes.</li>
    <li><strong>Tenancy in Common:</strong> Looking for flexibility? Enter tenancy in common. This setup allows uneven ownership splits, which can work better if one partner contributes more to the down payment or monthly mortgage. However, don't expect the same tidy convenience—if one of you passes away, the ownership share doesn’t go to the surviving partner but is instead distributed according to the deceased’s will.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Still wondering, <strong>"can an unmarried couple buy a house together"</strong> without complications? You sure can, as long as you dive into these ownership options in advance and solidify your game plan.</p>
  
  <h2>The Power of Cohabitation Agreements</h2>
  <p>Cyberpunk realty pro-tip: protect your investment like it’s gold-pressed latinum stashed in your high-security vault. Go beyond just relying on a handshake agreement or romantic vibes; get yourself a <strong>cohabitation agreement</strong>. Think of it as your partnership’s user manual for homeownership. This document should detail how everything from ownership percentages to repair cost responsibilities will shake out. Tackling these legal aspects isn’t just for pessimists—it's for planners who want to dodge chaos in the future.</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Ownership Percentages:</strong> Nail down whether you’re splitting ownership evenly or skewing it based on financial contributions. Transparency is your ally here.</li>
    <li><strong>Cost Sharing:</strong> Who's paying for what? Discuss and document how you'll divide mortgage payments, utility bills, insurance, and maintenance expenses.</li>
    <li><strong>Exit Strategy:</strong> Hate to play the “what-if” game, but breakups or changes in partnership are real scenarios. Outline who keeps the property and who gets bought out, or whether you both sell and split the profit.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>A cohabitation agreement transforms the question <strong>"can an unmarried couple buy a house together"</strong> into a roadmap for smooth financial sailing—regardless of what the future holds.</p>
