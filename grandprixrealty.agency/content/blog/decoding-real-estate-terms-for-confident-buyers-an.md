<h3>“The Market is Hot Right Now”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “More buyers are hunting than there are homes available, making competition fierce.”</p></blockquote>

<p>When agents say the market is "hot," they mean homes are selling faster than neon lights flicker in Night City. This could mean bidding wars, escalating prices, and cash offers ruling the game. If you're selling, it's an exciting time—you might receive multiple offers above asking price. If you're buying, gear up with a solid strategy and be ready to act fast.</p>

<h3>“This Home Has Great Bones”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “The structure is solid, but expect some updates.”</p></blockquote>

<p>Imagine you find a sleek high-rise condo with outdated cybernetic interfaces. The framework is perfect, but the interior needs an overhaul. That’s exactly what real estate agents mean when they say a property has "great bones." It means the fundamentals—like the foundation and layout—are strong. Cosmetic updates, like flooring, paint, or fixtures, can be modified to fit your style.</p>

<h3>“We’re Seeing Multiple Offers”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “There’s heavy competition, so your offer needs to stand out.”</p></blockquote>

<p>When demand surges, multiple offers are the norm. If you’re a buyer, this means going in with your best foot forward—think higher offer prices, fewer contingencies, and possibly an escalation clause to outbid competitors. Sellers benefit in this kind of landscape, with the ability to weigh top-tier offers and negotiate the best possible terms.</p>

<h3>“This Home is Priced to Sell”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “It’s a competitive price set for a quick sale.”</p></blockquote>

<p>When a home is “priced to sell,” it usually means it’s listed at or slightly below market value to generate immediate interest. The seller wants to move quickly, and it's likely to get snapped up fast. As a buyer, be prepared to act swiftly. As a seller, expect a flurry of activity and potential multiple offers.</p>

<h3>“Let’s Talk Contingencies”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “Let’s discuss contract conditions that protect your investment.”</p></blockquote>

<p>Contingencies are conditions that must be met before the sale is finalized. This could be anything from inspections revealing a hidden issue to financing approvals. Buyers typically include contingencies for home inspections and appraisals. On the flip side, sellers might push for fewer contingencies to speed up the deal.</p>

<h3>“Escrow”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “A neutral third party holds onto your money and documents until the deal is done.”</p></blockquote>

<p>Escrow acts as the digital vault that secures assets in a real estate transaction. Buyers deposit funds, which remain in a third-party account while all conditions—inspections, financing, and contingencies—are satisfied. Think of it as a safeguard that ensures both parties meet their obligations before the final handshake.</p> 

<h3>“Earnest Money Deposit”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “A good-faith payment proving you’re serious about buying.”</p></blockquote>

<p>When making an offer, buyers often front a portion of their payment known as earnest money. This deposit is held in escrow and applies to the final price at closing. If the buyer backs out for reasons outside of agreed-upon contingencies, they risk losing this amount. Sellers see earnest money as an assurance that the buyer is committed to sealing the deal.</p> 

<h3>“Appraisal”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “A neutral evaluation of the home’s value to keep the lender in check.”</p></blockquote>

<p>Appraisals are a bank’s way of ensuring a home isn’t overvalued. An independent appraiser examines comparable sales, the home’s condition, and market trends to determine its fair market value. If an appraisal comes in lower than the agreed-upon price, buyers and sellers might need to renegotiate or find extra funds to bridge the gap.</p>

<h3>“Contingencies”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “Protection clauses that allow you to back out under certain conditions.”</p></blockquote>

<p>Contingencies are conditions that must be met before the deal can move forward. Common examples include financing contingencies (ensuring the buyer secures a loan), home inspection contingencies (confirming the property has no serious defects), and appraisal contingencies (verifying the home’s value is aligned with the loan amount). These safety nets help buyers navigate potential pitfalls without taking unnecessary risks.</p>

<h3>“Closing Costs”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “All the extra fees it takes to finalize your home deal.”</p></blockquote>

<p>Beyond the sale price, buyers and sellers encounter an array of additional costs before crossing the finish line. These include loan origination fees, title insurance, escrow charges, and taxes. Typically, buyers cover most of these costs, though sellers may contribute depending on local practices and negotiations.</p>

<h3>“Title” & “Title Insurance”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “A legal document proving ownership—protected by insurance in case of disputes.”</p></blockquote>

<p>The title is the official record stating who legally owns a property. Title insurance is a crucial safeguard that protects buyers from potential ownership disputes, hidden liens, or clerical errors. Before the sale closes, a title company conducts a thorough search to uncover any unresolved claims on the property.</p>

<h3>“HOA Fees”</h3>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote"><p><strong>Translation:</strong> “Monthly payments for community maintenance and benefits.”</p></blockquote>

<p>If you’re buying in a condo or planned community, expect a Homeowners Association (HOA) fee in exchange for shared amenities, landscaping, and building upkeep. These fees vary based on location, property type, and included amenities, ranging from basic trash collection to full-service security and recreation facilities.</p>
