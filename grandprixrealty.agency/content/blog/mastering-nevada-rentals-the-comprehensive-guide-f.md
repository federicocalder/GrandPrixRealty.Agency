<h2>Summary of Nevada Rentals Laws NRS 118A </h2>

<p>The NRS118A is the legal text detailing the landlord and tenant laws for dwellings in Nevada. It covers various aspects of the landlord-tenant relationship, including definitions, rights, obligations, and legal procedures. Some of the key points from the document include:</p>

<p>Nevada Landlord and Tenant Laws: An Overview</p>

<p>Nevada has specific laws that govern the rights and responsibilities of landlords and tenants when it comes to renting a dwelling. Here are some key things to know:</p>

<h2>Definitions</h2>

<ul>
<li class="has-medium-font-size">"Landlord" refers to the owner of a dwelling that is rented to a tenant.</li>

<li class="has-medium-font-size">"Tenant" is the person who rents and occupies the dwelling.</li>

<li class="has-medium-font-size">"Dwelling" can be a house, apartment, mobile home, or other structure meant for people to live in.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Rental Agreements</h2>

<ul>
<li class="has-medium-font-size">Rental agreements must be in writing and contain information like the rent amount, occupancy rules, and landlord/tenant responsibilities. Landlords must provide tenants with a copy of the agreement.</li>

<li class="has-medium-font-size">Month-to-month tenancies are assumed if there is no set rental term. Landlords can charge late fees for overdue rent.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Rights and Responsibilities</h2>

<ul>
<li class="has-medium-font-size">Landlords must maintain dwellings in a habitable condition and comply with housing, building, and health codes. Tenants can terminate their lease if significant health/safety violations are not fixed.</li>

<li class="has-medium-font-size">Tenants must pay rent on time, keep the dwelling clean and safe, and repair any damages they or guests cause.</li>

<li class="has-medium-font-size">Landlords can adopt reasonable rules about tenants' use of the property. Rules must promote safety and fairness.</li>

<li class="has-medium-font-size">Landlords must store tenants' abandoned property for 30 days before disposing of it.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Ending a Lease</h2>

<ul>
<li class="has-medium-font-size">Tenants can end a periodic tenancy by giving proper written notice 30 days or more in advance.</li>

<li class="has-medium-font-size">Landlords can end a periodic tenancy without cause by giving notice 60 days or more in advance.</li>

<li class="has-medium-font-size">Landlords can start eviction proceedings if tenants don't pay rent or violate the rental agreement. There are rules about the eviction process.</li>
</ul>

<p>These are some of the basics of Nevada landlord-tenant law. The full law contains additional details about security deposits, discrimination, domestic violence protections, and more. Understanding these laws is important for both rental housing providers and tenants.</p>

<h2>Eviction and Relief:</h2>

<p>The court can conduct a hearing on a verified complaint for expedited relief within 3 judicial days after filing. If the landlord is found to have violated certain provisions, the court may order the landlord to restore the premises or essential items to the tenant, award damages, and possibly hold the landlord in contempt of court.</p>

<h2>Damage or Destruction:</h2>

<p>The law mentions provisions related to the damage or destruction of a dwelling unit by fire or other casualties.</p>

<h2>Disclosure Requirements:</h2>

<p>Landlords have certain obligations to disclose information to tenants. If they fail to do so, tenants may be entitled to recover damages.</p>

<h2>Tenant's Compliance:</h2>

<p>The Law discusses the consequences if a tenant fails to comply with the rental agreement or does not fulfill their basic obligations.</p>

<h2>Emergency Assistance:</h2>

<p>Landlords are prohibited from taking adverse actions against tenants solely based on the tenant's request for emergency assistance.</p>

<h2>Definitions: </h2>

<p>The law provides definitions for various terms related to landlord-tenant relationships, such as "exclude," "federal worker," "landlord," "normal wear," "owner," and "periodic rent."</p>

<h2><strong>FAQ on Retaliation Protections (NRS 118A.510):</strong></h2>

<h3>Q. What does NRS 118A.510 state about retaliation protections? </h3>

<p>A. NRS 118A.510 protects tenants from retaliatory actions by landlords. Landlords cannot retaliate against tenants for exercising their rights.</p>

<h3>Q. Can a landlord evict a tenant for reporting a violation or requesting repairs?</h3>

<p><strong>A:</strong> No, landlords cannot retaliate or take adverse actions against tenants for exercising their rights, such as reporting violations or requesting essential repairs.</p>

<h3><strong>Q:</strong> What can a tenant do if they believe their landlord is retaliating against them?</h3>

<p><strong>A:</strong> If a tenant believes they are being retaliated against, they should seek legal counsel. The law protects tenants from unjust retaliatory actions, and they may have grounds for legal recourse.</p>

<h2>Legal Procedures and Notifications</h2>

<p>One of the first things you should be aware of is the legal procedures and notifications. If you're a licensee, permittee, or owner-developer, you must file an answer to any charges within 30 days after receiving notice. And how is notice considered complete? It's either upon personal delivery or mailing by certified mail to your last known address. So, make sure your contact information is up-to-date to avoid any legal complications.</p>

<h2>Duties and Responsibilities of Licensees</h2>

<p>As a real estate professional, you have certain duties and responsibilities to uphold. You're required to exercise reasonable skill and care in carrying out the terms of the brokerage agreement. Confidentiality is key; you must not disclose any confidential information relating to a client for one year after the termination of the brokerage agreement. Also, you're obligated to present all offers made to or by the client as soon as practicable. Failure to adhere to these guidelines can result in legal repercussions.</p>

<h2>Asset Management and Disclosures</h2>

<p>If you're a broker providing asset management services, you have additional responsibilities. You must disclose such agreements annually to the Division and provide proof that you've complied with the requirements of NRS 645H.490. This ensures transparency and accountability in your operations.</p>

<h2>Discrimination and Fair Practices</h2>

<p>Discrimination has no place in the real estate industry. Discriminatory practices based on race, religious creed, color, national origin, disability, sexual orientation, gender identity or expression, ancestry, familial status, or sex are strictly unlawful. Make sure you're treating all clients fairly and equally.</p>

<h2>Advance Fees and Accounting</h2>

<p>Charging or collecting an advance fee? You must furnish an accounting of the use of that money within three months. This is crucial for maintaining trust and transparency with your clients.</p>

<h2>Administrative Details</h2>

<p>Last but not least, the document outlines the administrative structure, including the roles and responsibilities of the Real Estate Commission and the Real Estate Division. These bodies are essential for the governance and regulation of real estate practices in Nevada.</p>
